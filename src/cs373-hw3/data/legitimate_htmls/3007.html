Is It Safe?
Brasilia, BrazilPresident Dilma Rousseff, accompanied by Chief of Staff Jaques Wagner, looks out from a window at Planalto Palace in May 11; the following day senators voted to begin an impeachment trial against Rousseff and suspended her from Brazil's highest office. For months senators have lobbed accusations that Rousseff used accounting tricks to mask a growing budget deficit; after about 20 hours of debate they moved to pursue charges on May 12. "It's a coup," Rousseff told reporters following the news, pledging she would fight to retain her position as she criticized the all-white, all-male cabinet unveiled by acting President Michel Temer, a former ally who has become critical of Rousseff. I regret there is no woman or black person in the administration after many years, Rousseff said. Hours later Temer took to the airwaves to justify the change, saying "I am convinced that it is necessary to rescue Brazilians' trust internally and internationally."
